The Rise and Fall of Planet Hollywood

sharkweek 2021-06-14 06:13:57 +0000 UTC [ - ]

Random story about my experience going to Planet Hollywood as a kid!

When I was a pre-teen, I used to always ask restaurants/fast food places etc if the soda came with free refills. I didn’t even drink a ton of soda growing up, but it was something I always asked and associated with better experiences while dining out when I was younger. If a restaurant had free refills, it was alright in my book.

The first and only time (I think?) I ate at a Planet Hollywood I asked the server if there were free refills and instead of yes/no, they said, “you get nine refills at no charge.”

I distinctly remember his business-like tone when he said it, so I believed this was part of Planet Hollywood’s model: up to nine free refills of soda.

I don’t know why I’m sharing this other than that it’s late and it’s such a weird thing to remember so vividly from childhood.

cogman10 2021-06-14 17:50:38 +0000 UTC [ - ]

Yeah, it's doom to fail.

The problem a lot of chain restaurants are facing is the prevalence of the internet and online reviews. They are setup such that you can either go to a known ok place to eat vs some new experience that may or may not be good. However, finding a good place to eat is now easier than ever. I'll look for the type of food I want, then look at some restaurant reviews and I'm off.

Chains like Olive garden or Applebees struggle because every city probably has an Italian or American restaurant that's better quality and price. At this point their main clientele are the internet illiterate.

This is what has killed a place like Planet Hollywood. The entire appeal of it is to be sold to young people as a hip place to eat some good food... but young people are the most likely to read and leave reviews which doesn't bode well when you are serving terrible food. But also serving terrible food at a huge markup? That's a chain killer.

In the next 10 years, I expect that the only mid->high price chains that survive are the ones that adapt. Even then, the outlook is bleak. You aren't going to eat at Chili's when there's a local steakhouse at a similar price and higher quality.

pfdietz 2021-06-14 12:25:12 +0000 UTC [ - ]

Sometimes you get a great company arising out of the ashes of a dead company.

The example I'm thinking of is Nucor Steel. They arose from a company called Nuclear Corporation of America, a 60s era conglomerate that ultimately failed and was broken up. A part that was viable made steel roof elements for industrial buildings. Through a series of risky investments they built a small minimill to remelt scrap to make I beams and such, and this eventually grew into the largest US steel company, with $20B in revenue in 2020.

Interestingly, they also have the old REO Motor Company in their corporate ancestry. The CEO of Nucor in its early days would sometimes get calls from people looking for spare parts for old REO vehicles (including the REO Speed Wagon, a light truck the band was named after.)

I'm assuming SPACs would not count, as this is just using the zombie shell of a dead company to do what is effectively an IPO of a new company.

ascagnel_ 2021-06-14 15:44:11 +0000 UTC [ - ]

I feel like Gamestop is tee'd up to be the next Blockbuster -- a leader that utterly dominated a market that no longer exists due to digital delivery mechanisms.

- The two newest consoles (PS5 and Xbox Series) feature all-digital models that effectively neuter Gamestop's primary used-game market.

- Microsoft is pushing Game Pass over individual purchases, which cuts Gamestop out of new game sales.

- Free-to-play games allow Gamestop to sell prepaid currency cards, but there's nothing special about GS doing that versus any other store that sells gift cards.

- Customers generally have preferred digital distribution over in-store pickups, since the former can message discounts more effectively and make for snap impulse purchases.
